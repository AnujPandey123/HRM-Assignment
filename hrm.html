Ben Whittaker, Day 1

Ben Whittaker is a 70-year-old retired executive who re-enters the workforce through a senior intern program at a fast-growing e-commerce startup. A product of the Mad Men era — punctual, suited, briefcase-toting — he initially appears to be a fish out of water among hoodie-wearing millennials. But what unfolds is a masterclass in reverse mentorship, institutional wisdom, and the raw human power of showing up, listening hard, and caring deeply.

Ben's current title, "Senior Intern," is a glorified placeholder. It captures none of his actual value to the organisation. This document redesigns his role from scratch.

Section 02
HRM vs SHRM vs Personnel Management: Ben as a Test Case
🔍
Three Lenses, One Person: Personnel Management, HRM, and Strategic HRM would each treat Ben very differently. The approach shifts from reactive to proactive to strategic as we move across the spectrum — and the difference between them determines whether Ben's 40 years of experience get used or wasted.
📁 Personnel Management

How it sees Ben:

A temporary hire filling a defined headcount slot. His tasks are administrative, his value is operational, and his contract ends when the internship period concludes. He is a cost to be managed, not a human to be developed.

Result: Ben sorts emails and drives colleagues around. His 40 years of experience go untouched.

🤝 Human Resource Management

How it sees Ben:

A human asset whose wellbeing, skills, and engagement matter. HRM would invest in onboarding him properly, assign a buddy, track his satisfaction, and ensure he feels valued as an individual contributor regardless of age.

Result: Ben is integrated into team culture and respected. His mentoring emerges organically but informally.

🎯 Strategic HRM (SHRM)

How it sees Ben:

A rare source of competitive human capital. SHRM aligns Ben's unique KSAOs with the organisation's strategic gaps, designs a formal role, sets measurable outcomes, and positions him as a culture driver who supports Jules' leadership development.

Result: The redesigned role you will see in this document. This is the version that creates organisational value.

Job Title

Senior Experience Catalyst
Alternatively: Chief Wisdom Officer, People Anchor, or Generational Intelligence Advisor

Department

Reports to the CEO (Jules Ostin). Cross-functional liaison across People Ops, Product, and the Executive Office.

Employment Type

Part-time and flexible. 3 to 4 days per week. On-site preferred with hybrid acceptable. Renewable contract with semi-annual performance review.

Job Purpose

To embed seasoned professional judgment, cross-generational empathy, and emotional steadiness into a high-growth startup ecosystem, bridging lived business experience with modern agile culture.

Core Responsibilities

  • Serve as informal executive sounding board for the CEO
  • Mentor employees on professional norms and emotional regulation
  • Mediate interpersonal and cross-team tension
  • Advise on decisions using historical business precedent
  • Model sustained work ethic and professional standards
  • Document institutional learnings and cultural norms

What This Role Is NOT

  • An administrative assistant or errand runner
  • A token diversity hire for optics or PR
  • A technical specialist or product owner
  • A replacement for HR or formal counseling services
  • A passive observer or ceremonial figurehead

🤖 AI in Recruitment: The Irony

Most AI-driven Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) would have automatically rejected Ben's application. Why?

His most recent role was from a company that no longer exists. His skills — wisdom, empathy, institutional knowledge — do not appear in keyword searches. His "gap year" was actually 13 years of retirement.

This is a real-world critique: AI in recruitment optimises for pattern-matching, not for the kind of human value Ben represents. A thoughtful recruiter adds the judgment that an algorithm cannot replicate.

🏷️ Employer Branding: The Promise vs The Reality

The Promise They Made

About The Fit marketed itself as innovative and inclusive. The Senior Intern program was their employer branding statement: "We welcome everyone, even a 70-year-old."

The Reality Gap

Ben was initially ignored, assigned busywork, and excluded from conversations he could have meaningfully shaped. The employer brand promised inclusion but the HRM system delivered tokenism.

The Lesson

An HR professional's job is to close the gap between the employer brand promise and the lived employee experience. Without proper job design and talent integration, even the best branding becomes dishonest.

Section 12
What Ben Teaches Us About HRM
🏛️
HRM Without Ethics

HRM Without Ethics Is Just Administration

About The Fit's Senior Intern programme was well-intentioned but ethically incomplete. True SHRM requires asking whether HR practices respect human dignity, maximise human potential, and avoid tokenism. Ben's near-wasted talent is a lesson in what happens when organisations prioritise optics over genuine inclusion.

🗂️
Job Analysis as Strategy

Job Analysis Is Not Paperwork. It Is Strategy.

If About The Fit had conducted a proper job analysis before placing Ben, they would have designed a role that matched his actual capabilities to their actual organisational needs. The gap between what Ben was asked to do and what he actually contributed is exactly the gap that good job design closes.

🤖
Recruitment Limits

AI Can Screen Candidates. It Cannot See Wisdom.

Modern recruitment technology would have rejected Ben at the screening stage. His career gaps, old-world job titles, and absence of buzzwords would have filtered him out instantly. Knowing when to override the algorithm and apply human judgment is one of the most critical skills in HR today.

🔄
Learning Organisations

Learning Goes Both Ways in a True Learning Organisation

Most L&D frameworks assume the organisation develops the employee. Ben's case shows the reverse: the employee can develop the organisation. About The Fit became more emotionally intelligent, more patient, and more self-aware because of Ben. That is what a true Learning Organisation looks like.

💼
The Takeaway

Job Analysis Is Not Just About Tasks. It Is About Recognising All Forms of Human Value.

Traditional job analysis frameworks were built for roles that produce measurable outputs. Ben's role produces something harder to quantify: trust, psychological safety, institutional continuity, and cross-generational cohesion. Ben Whittaker deserved a role with a real title, real metrics, and real recognition. And so do millions of people like him in organisations around the world.
